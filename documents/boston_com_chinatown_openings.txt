URL: https://www.boston.com/food/

Boston's Chinatown continues to be the city's best neighborhood for an affordable, satisfying meal. Here are the spots readers and staff return to again and again.

Gourmet Dumpling House remains a perennial favorite for soup dumplings and pan-fried buns. Expect a wait at lunch and dinner, but the line moves and the prices stay friendly. A reader-favorite for cheap, filling group meals, and the scallion pancakes and dumplings rarely top a modest bill even for a table of four. Cash is the easiest way to pay.

Hei La Moon draws crowds for weekend dim sum served from rolling carts — an inexpensive way to sample a lot of dishes at once. Go before noon on Saturday or Sunday for the widest selection and the liveliest room. The baked pork buns and har gow are the dishes readers mention most.

China Pearl is the other big dim sum name, often recommended interchangeably with Hei La Moon. Cavernous, busy, and great value for a big group brunch.

For a quick bite, the Beach Street bakeries sell pork buns and egg tarts for a couple of dollars, and several pho shops serve big bowls for under $15. A Vietnamese iced coffee and a bowl of pho is one of the best cheap, warming meals downtown.

Noodle and rice plates. Beyond dumplings and dim sum, the neighborhood's casual spots do hand-pulled noodles, congee, and clay-pot rice dishes that fill you up for not much money. These are the everyday meals locals lean on, not just the weekend treats.

Late-night and value tips. Chinatown is one of the few parts of downtown where you can still get a hot, cheap meal late at night. Bring cash where you can, expect short lines at the most popular spots (a good sign), and don't overlook the bakeries for a cheap breakfast or snack.

The throughline: in Chinatown, you can eat very well for very little, and the names that come up most are Gourmet Dumpling House for soup dumplings, Hei La Moon and China Pearl for dim sum, and the Beach Street bakeries for cheap snacks on the go.
