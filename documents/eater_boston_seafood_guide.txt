Neptune Oyster (North End). The tiny North End raw bar remains the standard-bearer for lobster rolls in the city. Order the hot buttered version — warm lobster meat folded with drawn butter on a griddled bun — which regulars argue is the single best bite of seafood in Boston. Prices run high (the lobster roll hovers around the mid-$30s) and the restaurant does not take reservations, so expect a long wait, especially at peak hours. The consensus among diners: expensive and crowded, but worth it for a special meal. Put your name in early, grab a drink nearby, and don't skip the raw oysters while you wait.

Row 34 (Seaport). A more modern, spacious option with a polished raw bar and a cold, classic-style lobster roll dressed lightly in mayo. Easier to get a table than Neptune, and a reliable choice in the Seaport for a sit-down seafood dinner. The oyster selection is deep and the beer list is a draw in its own right. A good pick when you want quality without the North End crush.

James Hook & Co. (Waterfront). The no-frills, order-at-the-counter pick. Cheaper than the sit-down spots, generous on lobster, and best eaten outside by the water on a warm day. Great value if you don't care about ambiance — this is the spot for a quick, satisfying roll without the wait or the markup.

Saltie Girl (Back Bay). A stylish small-plates seafood bar known for its lobster roll and an over-the-top "lobster tower." Pricey and often packed, but a fun, design-forward option if you're staying near Newbury Street. The torched-butter lobster roll has a devoted following.

Yankee Lobster (Seaport). A family-run fish market and casual restaurant near the convention center. Counter service, paper plates, and honest portions at fair prices. Locals send visitors here when they want the real, unpretentious version of a Boston seafood shack.

Union Oyster House (Faneuil Hall). The historic, touristy institution — America's oldest continuously operating restaurant. Reviews are mixed: some love the history and the clam chowder, others find it overpriced and coasting on its landmark status. Go for the atmosphere and the chowder, temper expectations on everything else.

Cold vs. hot lobster roll. The eternal Boston debate. The classic Maine-style roll is cold lobster bound in a little mayo on a toasted, split-top bun. The Connecticut-style roll is warm lobster in melted butter. Neptune's hot buttered version is the one most often cited as the city's best, but plenty of purists hold out for a clean, cold, mayo-light roll where the lobster speaks for itself.

Bottom line. For best-in-class flavor, Neptune Oyster wins despite the price and the wait. For value, James Hook or Yankee Lobster. For comfort and availability, Row 34. For a night out near Newbury, Saltie Girl. There is no wrong neighborhood for seafood in Boston, but the North End and Seaport are where most of the conversation happens.
